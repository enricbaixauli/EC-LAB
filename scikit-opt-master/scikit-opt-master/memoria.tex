\subsection{Methodology}
\subsubsection{Parameter Sweep Strategy}
For these experiments, we decided to follow a sequential approach for parameter tuning. Instead of making a grid search with all possible combinations of the variables' fixed values, we set up an order of exploration, reducing significantly the computational cost the grid could bring. 

Our criteria was to first test the variables that could impact the results the most, then pick their best values and set them for the remaining experimentation. 
The order chosen was the following: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{w, c1 and c2 / F and CR}
    \item \textbf{Population size} (prov. value = 50)
    \item \textbf{Number of iterations} (prov. value = 150)
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Global Values}
In order to be coherent with the experiments, a set of values was defined regarding the different fitness functions and the difficulty of the tasks. For the \textbf{Griewank} function, we decided to bound the dominion to the standard \textbf{[-600, 600]}, on the other hand we reduced that variable significantly to \textbf{[-5, 5]} for \textbf{Rosenbrock} due to its difficult nature. Additionally, the predetermined \textbf{number of dimensions} was set to \textbf{10} in order to better analyse the behaviour of the algorithms thanks to a harder task.

The number of \textbf{Monte Carlo runs} was also set to \textbf{30} for all experiments.

\subsubsection{Conclusions on PSO}
Along this section, we have been able to see the performance of the PSO algorithm on two different fitness functions, with different natures and difficulties. We have seen how the parameters of the algorithm can greatly impact its performance, and how finding the right balance between exploration and exploitation is crucial for achieving good results.
In the Griewank function, which is a multimodal function with many local minimums, we found that the best configuration was one with low inertia and higher cognitive component, which allowed the particles to explore more independently and avoid getting stuck in local minimums. In the Rosenbrock function, which is a unimodal function with a narrow valley, we found that the best configuration was one with even lower inertia, which allowed the particles to navigate the steep valley more effectively.
In both functions, we also saw that increasing the population size generally led to better performance, as it allowed for a more diverse search of the solution space and reduced the chances of getting stuck in local minimums. Additionally, we observed that increasing the number of iterations can lead to better results, but there may be a point of diminishing returns where additional iterations do not lead to significant improvements in fitness.
Overall, the PSO algorithm showed good performance on both functions, but the choice of parameters was crucial for achieving good results, and the optimal parameters varied depending on the nature of the fitness function being optimized.

        \item \textbf{F and CR}
        
            In DE, the variable F is the mutation factor, which controls how much the mutant vector is influenced by the difference between two randomly selected individuals. A higher F encourages more exploration, while a lower F promotes exploitation. The variable CR is the crossover rate, which determines the probability of components being inherited from the mutant vector versus the target vector. A higher CR allows for more mixing of solutions, while a lower CR leads to more conservative updates.

\subsubsection{Population Size}



Beginning with the population size analysis, because in this problem the search space for the berlin52 dataset is dramatically large ($52!$ possible permutations), we expected, and got, a direct correlation between a larger population size and a reduction in the relative error of the discovered tours.

We can appreciate, based on the results, how with a small population of 20 individuals, the algorithm lacks the genetic diversity needed to fully explore the permutation space and frequently gets trapped in local minimums, yielding a high average error of $13.51\%$ and a high maximum error of $20.75\%$.

As the population is increased to the default 50, and then massively to 200, the performance improves significantly. A population of 200 achieves the best average error ($8.58\%$) and dramatically lowers the minimum error found across the 30 runs down to an impressive $2.48\%$. Furthermore, the standard deviation narrows to $3.22$, and the maximum error drops to $16.87\%$, indicating higher reliability. Theoretically, this occurs because with more population comes more genetic diversity, and therefore a wider variety of valid city permutations. This extended spatial coverage, gives the crossover operators a richer set of parental edge sequences, allowing the algorithm to gather optimal sub-tours much more effectively without suffering from premature convergence.

In comparison to the baseline heuristics, while a population of 20 only confidently beats the Closest-Neighbor approach ($24.10\%$), scaling the population to 200 allows the Genetic Algorithm to consistently outperform the Quick Tour heuristic ($10.78\%$) on average, and occasionally rival the highly optimized Pair-Center heuristics in its best runs.

\subsubsection{Number of Generations}



With the results of the different values of this hyperparameter, we have observed the importance of allowing the algorithm sufficient time to assemble optimal sub-tours (building blocks) into a complete, valid route.

We can appreciate, the disastrous result of the performance when the model is restricted to only 100 generations, with an astonishing average relative error of $57.64\%$, with a massive standard deviation of $8.07$ and maximum errors reaching $75.07\%$. For the big size of this problem, 100 generations are simply insufficient for the crossover and mutation operators to really optimize the results.

With 500 generations, there's a remarkable improvement and this shows how, given adequate time to exploit the genetic material, the algorithm correctly pieces together the local optimal edges, causing the average error to plummet to $10.49\%$. The standard deviation also narrows significantly to $3.10$, indicating a much more stable and predictable convergence pattern.

Finally, with 2000 generations, the overall average error diminishes considerably less (as it was expected) but this reduction in the error is probably due to micro-adjustments in the elite individuals. Nevertheless, the minimum error achieved across the runs hits an extraordinary $1.70\%$. This specific run is of immense significance because it officially outperforms the highly optimized Pair-Center Quick heuristic ($1.74\%$). This proves that, given enough generational time, an evolutionary approach can beat deterministic heuristics. 

\subsubsection{Mutation Probability}



In the graph above we can see how applying a near-zero mutation rate ($0.01$) to the TSP results in catastrophic failure, yielding an abysmal average error of $72.63\%$ and a massive standard deviation of $11.44$.
These results were unexpected at first, given the tendency we saw in the first problem of a better performance the lower the mutation rate was. But after some thought, and focusing on the change of paradigm of problem 2, it started to make sense.

In continuous optimization, tiny mutations act like small nudges that help refine a solution smoothly, but in the TSP the search space is made of permutations, so small tweaks don’t fix a fundamentally bad route, you need large structural changes to escape poor city orders. Because crossover in TSP tends to produce children very similar to their parents, a low mutation rate quickly makes the whole population nearly identical, causing premature convergence. 

The data shows that to break out of these rigid local minima, the TSP requires highly aggressive structural changes, that's why increasing the mutation probability to $0.10$ drastically reduces the average error to $19.41\%$ and maximizing the mutation rate to a fully aggressive $1.00$ yields the best performance among the three values tested, lowering the average error to $14.70\%$, the minimum error to $8.00\%$, and stabilizing the standard deviation to $3.90$.

\subsubsection{Tournament Size}



As expected, reducing the tournament size to 1 results in a poor average relative error of $14.75\%$ and a high standard deviation ($4.11$), this is due to the selection process becoming entirely random.

Meanwhile, we can appreciate how restoring the tournament size to a moderate value of 3 provides an optimal evolutionary balance. This configuration yields a robust average error of $9.44\%$ and crucially, it achieves the absolute best minimum error among the three tested tournament values ($2.45\%$). Given these results, we can affirm that in this case the selection pressure is strong enough to propagate good sub-tours but gentle enough to maintain genetic diversity within the population.

Conversely, augmenting the tournament size to 10 didn't give a major improvement, while the average relative error what slightly better ($8.97\%$), the best run showed a bigger error. This could be the consequence of flooding the population with clones of the early elite individuals, in this case the algorithm suffers from a degree of premature convergence and its exploratory power is diminished, preventing it from discovering the absolute best global routes.

We basically combined all the values that performed the best during their hyperparameter's individual evaluation, tested it across 30 Monte Carlo rounds and directly compared it to the three baseline heuristics provided in the theoretical framework.
