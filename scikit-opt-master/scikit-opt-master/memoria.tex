\subsection{Methodology}
\subsubsection{Parameter Sweep Strategy}
For these experiments, we decided to follow a sequential approach for parameter tuning. Instead of making a grid search with all possible combinations of the variables' fixed values, we set up an order of exploration, reducing significantly the computational cost the grid could bring. 

Our criteria was to first test the variables that could impact the results the most, then pick their best values and set them for the remaining experimentation. 
The order chosen was the following: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{w, c1 and c2 / F and CR}
    \item \textbf{Population size} (prov. value = 50)
    \item \textbf{Number of iterations} (prov. value = 150)
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Global Values}
In order to be coherent with the experiments, a set of values was defined regarding the different fitness functions and the difficulty of the tasks. For the \textbf{Griewank} function, we decided to bound the dominion to the standard \textbf{[-600, 600]}, on the other hand we reduced that variable significantly to \textbf{[-5, 5]} for \textbf{Rosenbrock} due to its difficult nature. Additionally, the predetermined \textbf{number of dimensions} was set to \textbf{10} in order to better analyse the behaviour of the algorithms thanks to a harder task.

The number of \textbf{Monte Carlo runs} was also set to \textbf{30} for all experiments.

\paragraph{Discussion of Results}
\begin{itemize} 

    \item \textbf{Population Size}
    

    When observing the graphs above and, specifically, the fitness score followed by the generations, we can appreciate how the Average of Population Average follows a practically identical trajectory across all three population sizes, rapidly descending from the extreme high-penalty walls ($10^4$) and stabilizing around $10^3$ in the early generations.

    However, the population size does dictates the speed at which the elite individuals find the valley floor. With just a population of 20, the algorithm lacks parallel exploration power, that's the reason why the Average Best Fitness drops very gradually from $10^3$ and only stabilizes around $10^0$ in the final 10 generations. Meanwhile, with the default size of 50, this descent is much more efficient, stabilizing near $10^0$ by generation 40, and with a huge population of 1000 individuals, the optimal valley floor is located considerably faster, stabilizing by generation 20.

    Finally, even though the convergence plots suggest that larger populations converge more slowly, the final averages observed on the table shock with their results, here population 20 appears to perform best (0.5140), even beating population 50 (0.7882) and matching population 1000 (0.5286). But if we take a look at its enormous standard deviation (0.5988), we can quickly know why, in practice, the small population behaves chaotically: in some runs it gets lucky and starts near the optimum, and in others it fails completely. One of the advantages of augmenting the population is that this randomness disappears, for example by population 1000, the standard deviation shrinks dramatically (0.2897), meaning the algorithm reliably reaches the same low region every time. So while a huge population doesn’t magically break the flat floor of the binary Rosenbrock function, it does guarantee fast, stable, and repeatable convergence, something small populations simply cannot offer.
    \item \textbf{Mutation Probability}
    
     

    As it happened with the results from the Schaffer function, a mutation probability of 0.1 achieved really bad results, with no notable improvement between the metrics. This indicates that a 10\% mutation rate is far too aggressive, it constantly corrupts the genetic structure of the individuals, randomly throwing the vast majority of the population out of the optimal valley and high up into the steep walls of the Rosenbrock landscape.

    A good balance is found with the default $0.01$, there can be seen a highly effective evolutionary dynamic, the population average drops rapidly before stabilizing, while the best individuals steadily descend toward the optimum. Mutation is now strong enough to introduce new information but not so strong that it disrupts progress, allowing the algorithm to isolate the valley reliably and achieve the best final result among the three settings (0.8247).
    
    Finally, lowering mutation too far to 0.001 cripples the search. The population average collapses dangerously close to the best fitness early on, a clear sign of premature convergence and loss of diversity. With so little mutation, the bit‑string representation cannot generate the new directions needed to navigate the flat floor of the Rosenbrock function, causing the population to stall in different suboptimal regions across runs. This leads to slow, incomplete convergence and a weak final average of 1.9629, accompanied by a very large standard deviation that reflects how inconsistently the algorithm performs when genetic innovation is nearly absent.
    \item \textbf{Tournament Size}
    
     When the tournament size is reduced to 1, we already know the consequences: random search due to zero selective pressure. The results in this case are as bad as the previous ones seen in both Schaffer codings.
     

    On the other hand, a tournament size of 3 completely restores the evolutionary guidance. The Average of Population Average forms an "L" shape, dropping from over $10^4$ to $10^3$ early on and then stabilizing at that height. This stabilization is highly beneficial because it means the broader population continues to actively explore the upper slopes of the valley, preserving genetic diversity, while the elite individuals successfully exploit the central basin. Consequently, the Average Best Fitness drops gradually and smoothly from $10^3$ down to $10^0$ around generation 40, achieving a strong tabular result of $0.6657$.

    Interestingly, increasing the tournament size to 10 yields the best final average performance ($0.3881$), contrasting sharply with the Schaffer experiments where high selection pressure caused premature convergence. This can be explained because in the context of the Rosenbrock function, the hyper-aggressive selection pressure actually provides a strategic advantage, by aggressively favoring the few individuals that manage to fall into the optimal valley early on, the algorithm avoids wasting generations evaluating the high-penalty walls. This forces the Average Best Fitness to reach $10^0$ notably faster (around generation 36). While the slightly thicker standard deviation ($0.4447$) indicates that this aggressive strategy introduces a bit more variance across independent runs, the overall rapid descent mechanism proves highly effective for navigating Rosenbrock's steep terrain.
    \item \textbf{Precision}
    
    As with the Schaffer function under binary encoding, changing the precision does not alter the overall evolutionary behavior of the algorithm: all three precision settings produce nearly identical convergence curves, showing that the default configuration (population 50, mutation 0.01, tournament 3) is robust enough to guide the search into the Rosenbrock valley. 
    
    However, the final numerical results reveal that finer precision consistently provides better outcomes. With a resolution of 0.1, the algorithm achieves the weakest average best fitness (1.1660) because the binary grid is too rough to make the tiny adjustments required along the Rosenbrock valley floor. Even when the population reaches the basin, the chromosome simply lacks the decimal granularity needed to move closer to the true minimum. On the other hand, increasing precision to 0.001 dramatically improves the search resolution, allowing elite individuals to take much smaller, more accurate steps along the flat valley. As a result, the highest‑precision setting achieves the best final performance (0.7054), reflecting how essential a precise representation is for navigating the narrow and delicate structure of the Rosenbrock landscape.

\end{itemize}

Thanks to this massive exploration, the Average Best Fitness reaches the $10^0$ mark much earlier and continues to refine itself to an even lower value.     If we take a look at the plots we can observe how, this time around, a lower mutation does not improve in all metrics. At first glance, the visual plots for the Average Best Fitness appear almost identical across all three configurations, successfully descending and stabilizing between $10^0$ and $10^1$, with the standard deviation also following a visually similar stabilization pattern. However, if we focus on the Average of Population Average, the lower the mutation probability, the more erratic the population's behavior becomes, drawing even dangerously close to the elite fitness line.

    In this case, applying the lowest mutation rate of $0.001$ yields the worst tabular performance ($1.9106$) and a highly unstable standard deviation ($1.2212$). In the exceptionally narrow and curved Rosenbrock valley, a near-zero mutation rate prevents the injection of necessary genetic diversity., this is captured by the continuous, erratic upward spikes in the population average plot.

    Conversely, applying a notably high mutation rate of $0.1$ proves to be the most effective strategy for this specific topography, providing us with the best final tabular average ($1.1643$) and the most stable standard deviation ($0.6089$). The plot for this configuration also shows a much smoother Average of Population Average that maintains a safe, healthy distance from the elite fitness. By introducing a higher degree of continuous random perturbation, the algorithm prevents the population from completely collapsing into a single point, thus allowing the individuals to spread out just enough to safely navigate the curved, flat floor of the valley without constantly crashing into the steep walls.
    \item \textbf{Tournament Size}
    

    The results for the tournament with size 1 are no different from the ones that we have already seen. Thanks to it being basically a random search with no evolutionary guidance, the Average Best Fitness actually worsens slightly over time, resulting in an abysmal tabular average of 82.596 and a massive standard deviation of 54.061, also the Average of Population Average remains statically trapped at extreme penalty values around $10^5$.

    Interestingly, increasing the tournament size to 10 actively degrades the algorithm's efficiency, contrasting with the binary coding scenario where high pressure accelerated the descent. In real coding, a hyper-aggressive tournament size of 10 causes premature convergence. This can be explained because, due to the geometric characteristics of Rosenbrock, we need a gradual exploration to enter and follow the valley. With a tournament size of 10, the selective pressure is big because frequently the fittest individual of the population wins and the same individuals are repeatedly chosen, so the population turns homogeneous quickly and loses the genetic diversity to follow the valley.

\subsubsection{Conclusions on DE}
In contrast to the clear superiority of the real coding in the Schaffer function, in the optimization of the Rosenbrock function the binary coding is highly competitive and often superior. While the real coding requires a massive population of 1000 individuals to achieve an average fitness of 0.50, the binary achieves an excellent 0.38 by simply increasing the selective pressure, showing a much more stable convergence and free from the constant erratic peaks suffered by the real version.

This anomaly can be explained by the Rosenbrock topology, just as we explained in the tournament size section, and it is characterized by an extremely flat, narrow, and curved optimal valley. This shape has an effect because, in the real coding, continuous crossover operators generate offspring by interpolating straight lines between the parents, and if two elite parents are at different points on the curve, the straight line connecting them can cut the curve and place the child outside the valley, crashing it into the steep walls of high penalty. On the contrary, this does not happen in binary coding, whose crossover operators completely avoid this geometric trap because instead of tracing continuous lines, they progress in small nonlinear jumps along its mathematical grid, which helps to avoid the curvature of the valley and descend much more efficiently towards the global minimum.

\subsubsection{Population Size}



Beginning with the population size analysis, because in this problem the search space for the berlin52 dataset is dramatically large ($52!$ possible permutations), we expected, and got, a direct correlation between a larger population size and a reduction in the relative error of the discovered tours.

We can appreciate, based on the results, how with a small population of 20 individuals, the algorithm lacks the genetic diversity needed to fully explore the permutation space and frequently gets trapped in local minimums, yielding a high average error of $13.51\%$ and a high maximum error of $20.75\%$.

As the population is increased to the default 50, and then massively to 200, the performance improves significantly. A population of 200 achieves the best average error ($8.58\%$) and dramatically lowers the minimum error found across the 30 runs down to an impressive $2.48\%$. Furthermore, the standard deviation narrows to $3.22$, and the maximum error drops to $16.87\%$, indicating higher reliability. Theoretically, this occurs because with more population comes more genetic diversity, and therefore a wider variety of valid city permutations. This extended spatial coverage, gives the crossover operators a richer set of parental edge sequences, allowing the algorithm to gather optimal sub-tours much more effectively without suffering from premature convergence.

In comparison to the baseline heuristics, while a population of 20 only confidently beats the Closest-Neighbor approach ($24.10\%$), scaling the population to 200 allows the Genetic Algorithm to consistently outperform the Quick Tour heuristic ($10.78\%$) on average, and occasionally rival the highly optimized Pair-Center heuristics in its best runs.

\subsubsection{Number of Generations}



With the results of the different values of this hyperparameter, we have observed the importance of allowing the algorithm sufficient time to assemble optimal sub-tours (building blocks) into a complete, valid route.

We can appreciate, the disastrous result of the performance when the model is restricted to only 100 generations, with an astonishing average relative error of $57.64\%$, with a massive standard deviation of $8.07$ and maximum errors reaching $75.07\%$. For the big size of this problem, 100 generations are simply insufficient for the crossover and mutation operators to really optimize the results.

With 500 generations, there's a remarkable improvement and this shows how, given adequate time to exploit the genetic material, the algorithm correctly pieces together the local optimal edges, causing the average error to plummet to $10.49\%$. The standard deviation also narrows significantly to $3.10$, indicating a much more stable and predictable convergence pattern.

Finally, with 2000 generations, the overall average error diminishes considerably less (as it was expected) but this reduction in the error is probably due to micro-adjustments in the elite individuals. Nevertheless, the minimum error achieved across the runs hits an extraordinary $1.70\%$. This specific run is of immense significance because it officially outperforms the highly optimized Pair-Center Quick heuristic ($1.74\%$). This proves that, given enough generational time, an evolutionary approach can beat deterministic heuristics. 

\subsubsection{Mutation Probability}



In the graph above we can see how applying a near-zero mutation rate ($0.01$) to the TSP results in catastrophic failure, yielding an abysmal average error of $72.63\%$ and a massive standard deviation of $11.44$.
These results were unexpected at first, given the tendency we saw in the first problem of a better performance the lower the mutation rate was. But after some thought, and focusing on the change of paradigm of problem 2, it started to make sense.

In continuous optimization, tiny mutations act like small nudges that help refine a solution smoothly, but in the TSP the search space is made of permutations, so small tweaks don’t fix a fundamentally bad route, you need large structural changes to escape poor city orders. Because crossover in TSP tends to produce children very similar to their parents, a low mutation rate quickly makes the whole population nearly identical, causing premature convergence. 

The data shows that to break out of these rigid local minima, the TSP requires highly aggressive structural changes, that's why increasing the mutation probability to $0.10$ drastically reduces the average error to $19.41\%$ and maximizing the mutation rate to a fully aggressive $1.00$ yields the best performance among the three values tested, lowering the average error to $14.70\%$, the minimum error to $8.00\%$, and stabilizing the standard deviation to $3.90$.

\subsubsection{Tournament Size}



As expected, reducing the tournament size to 1 results in a poor average relative error of $14.75\%$ and a high standard deviation ($4.11$), this is due to the selection process becoming entirely random.

Meanwhile, we can appreciate how restoring the tournament size to a moderate value of 3 provides an optimal evolutionary balance. This configuration yields a robust average error of $9.44\%$ and crucially, it achieves the absolute best minimum error among the three tested tournament values ($2.45\%$). Given these results, we can affirm that in this case the selection pressure is strong enough to propagate good sub-tours but gentle enough to maintain genetic diversity within the population.

Conversely, augmenting the tournament size to 10 didn't give a major improvement, while the average relative error what slightly better ($8.97\%$), the best run showed a bigger error. This could be the consequence of flooding the population with clones of the early elite individuals, in this case the algorithm suffers from a degree of premature convergence and its exploratory power is diminished, preventing it from discovering the absolute best global routes.
